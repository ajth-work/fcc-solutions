Our Method

A list of required kanji was created using the available data on "http://peterkinnaird.eu/kani/#kanji" which shows a list of "N1" kanji not taught on WaniKani.

A further list was created using data from "https://www.wkstats.com/#charts.joyo" which expressly states the "173 of 2136 Jōyō kanji not yet covered by WaniKani."

After cross-referencing the two lists and removing any duplicate kanji, a final list of the kanji to be studied was created.

Every kanji on that final list had its associated meaning and readings collected and documented. The meanings used were the meanings given on: "www.jisho.org".

  • Note: There are some kanji which aren't part of either list however these were added due to be found in the vocabulary used in our content.

The "on" and "kun" readings were taken from a document created by the Japanese "Agency for Cultural Affairs" available here:

  • "http://www.bunka.go.jp/kokugo_nihongo/sisaku/joho/joho/kijun/naikaku/kanji/index.html”.

For any kanji not contained in this document, a different method was employed. The "on" and "kun" readings were taken from "http://kanji.jitenon.jp/" checked further with "https://kotobank.jp/word/" and with the pièce de résistance of having between 23 to 46 native Japanese speakers surveyed, confirming the readings.

Almost every kanji on this list has associated vocabulary for various parts of speech such as nouns or adjectives. The Jōyō kanji had several associated vocabulary published in the Jōyō Chart (常用表) document mentioned above. The majority of these were used in this resource.

For the kanji not contained, again, a different method was used. The kanji was searched on "www.jisho.org" and the most useable (in some cases, only available) vocabulary containing that kanji was placed into an online questionnaire. This questionnaire was distributed to upto 46 native Japanese speakers who stated whether they knew the vocabulary or not, and also added any additional vocabulary they thought might be useful. The questionnaire data was then analysed with the results sorted from well recognised vocabulary to poorly recognised vocabulary.

After ordering the vocabulary from most usable to least, the most useable was placed into the various units that comprise the memrise course.

All kanji have a meaning mnemonic, and where relevant, an on-reading mnemonic and/or kun-reading mnemonic.

The meaning mnemonic uses WaniKani radicals as well as several "compound radicals" (generally a standalone kanji like 龍 being used in 籠). Please feel free to make your own mnemonics.

Many of the different parts of speech have a mnemonic included where the reading may slightly differ to the basic "on/kun" readings.

Several of the mnemonics may be a little "NSFW" including swear words or vulgar imagery, so please bear that in mind as you study.

Level Breakdown

The kanji list is split into 6 primary units and each unit contains 60 kanji.

For each unit, there are 11 separate Memrise levels that cover the following:

  • Kanji
    • Meanings
    • On’yomi Readings
    • Kun’yomi Readings
  • Nouns
    • Meaning
    • Reading
  • Verbs
    • Meaning
    • Reading
  • Adjectives
    • Meaning
    • Reading
  • Other Parts of Speech (such as adverbs or expressions)
    • Meaning
    • Reading

While you don’t have to study in the aforementioned order, we do recommend that you first learn the three kanji levels for a given unit.

Mnemonics Breakdown

The Basics

Mnemonics are essentially sentences that use supporting material to build a memory around some target material. Mnemonics are referred to as “mems” on Memrise. You can read about Memrise’s information on mnemonics here.

Our course’s mnemonics generally follow a similar format. Due to the limitations of Memrise and being unable to use different colours for fonts, we use brackets () (American English: “parentheses”) to differentiate between supporting material and the target material.

  • Supporting Material is capitalized within brackets.
  • Target Material is capitalized without brackets.

Kanji

  • Example: 柿
    • Our mnemonic: “The only (TREE) in this (CITY) is a PERSIMMON tree.”
    • In this example, the target material is the meaning, “PERSIMMON”, for the kanji 「柿」.
    • In this example, the supporting materials are (TREE) and (CITY),
      • (TREE) and (CITY) are the “associated meanings” for the two radicals (木 and 市) that constitute the target material’s kanji「柿」.
    • Notice, the supporting materials, (TREE) and (CITY), are capitalized within brackets, and the target material, “PERSIMMON”, is capitalized without brackets.

Vocabulary

  • Example: 曖昧
    • Our mnemonic: “An idea that's (NOT CLEAR) and (FOOLISH) is VAGUE and AMBIGUOUS.”
    • In this example, the target material is the meaning, “VAGUE and AMBIGUOUS”, for the vocabulary term, 「曖昧」.
    • In this example, the supporting materials are (NOT CLEAR) and (FOOLISH).
      • (NOT CLEAR) and (FOOLISH) are the “primary meanings” for the two kanji (曖 and 昧) that constitute the target material’s vocabulary term 「曖昧」.
    • Notice, the supporting materials, (NOT CLEAR) and (FOOLISH), are capitalized within brackets, and the target materials, “VAGUE and AMBIGUOUS”, are capitalized without brackets.

“ON-reading” and “KUN-reading” mnemonics

There is a variety of styles used in writing the reading mnemonics. Whilst the majority follow a particular writing convention, there are exceptions. These exceptions are primarily down to creating what we believe to be the “easiest” way to remember the reading.

  • Example of regular mnemonic - format 1: 苛烈 (A008)
    • If you don't eat your かれつ(CARROTS) there will be a SEVERE punishment.
    • In this example the reading of the word, written in hiragana, comes BEFORE the English word being used to remember it. The target meaning for the vocabulary is in CAPITAL LETTERS without any brackets.
  • Example of regular mnemonic - format 2: 謁 (K013)
    • My ETCH(えつ)-a-sketch skills got me an AUDIENCE WITH the king.
    • The only difference for format 2 is that the reading of the word, written in hiragana, comes AFTER the English word being used to remember it. Again the target meaning is in CAPITAL LETTERS without any brackets.
  • Example of an exception: 謔 (K027)
    • The ぎゃく(逆) of being a scrooge is being a SPORT WITH a joke.
    • In this example the reading of ぎゃく doesn’t lend itself to any decent mnemonics through being similar to an English word. However, the same reading is used for the bracketed (逆). So, the mnemonic uses the meaning of 逆 which is taught on WaniKani.

Database Breakdown

We organized and tested our database to work inside Memrise exceedingly well, but we aren’t perfect.

Let us know at afterlife.kanji@gmail.com if there are any issues you come across as you use our course.

We created an external database within Google Sheets and we imported our information into Memrise’s database system.

If you weren’t already aware, within the Memrise database system, there are two types of columns:

  • “Columns”
    • Essentially, this is for testable content, such as Term, Meaning, or Reading.
  • “Attributes”
    • Basically, this is for relevant and convenient information that doesn’t really need to be tested, such as Part of Speech, Notes, etc.

As for our Memrise course’s overall database structure, we have the following “Columns”:

  • TERM
    • Typically, a kanji or vocabulary word.
  • RADICALS/COMPOUNDS
    • Radicals and Compound Radicals that make up the kanji.
    • Note: This column is only filled in on Kanji levels.
  • MEANING
    • The definition(s) of the TERM.
    • Note: To reduce length, we created a 60-character limit. Any additional MEANING content was moved to NOTES and is preceded by “Additional Meanings: ”.
  • MEANING MNEMONIC
    • A learning mnemonic to help remember the MEANING of the TERM.
  • READING (TERM)
    • The pronunciation of the TERM.
    • Note: This column is not filled in on Kanji levels.
  • READING MNEMONIC (TERM)
    • A learning mnemonic to help remember the pronunciation of the TERM.
    • Note: This column is not filled in on Kanji levels.
  • READING (ON)
    • The “on’yomi” (音読み) for a Kanji TERM.
    • On’yomi typically refers to the Chinese-influenced pronunciation of a kanji, therefore, we input on’yomi in katakana (the syllabary usually used for foreign, imported words).
    • Note: This column is only filled on Kanji levels.
  • READING MNEMONIC (ON)
    • A learning mnemonic to help remember the on’yomi pronunciation of a given kanji.
    • Note: This column is only filled on Kanji levels.
  • READING (KUN)
    • The “kun’yomi” (訓読み) for a Kanji TERM.
    • Kun’yomi typically refers to the Japanese pronunciation of a kanji, therefore, we input kun’yomi in hiragana (the syllabary usually used for native Japanese words).
    • Note: This column is only filled on Kanji levels.
  • READING MNEMONIC (KUN)
    • A learning mnemonic to help remember the kun’yomi pronunciation of a given kanji.
    • Note: This column is only filled on Kanji levels.

As well as the following “Attributes”:

  • #
    • Database Identification Number - a unique identification number allowing easy referencing and error reporting. For example, “N062” refers to “Noun 062”.
  • UNIT
    • Each kanji has a UNIT label detailing what Unit it came from, such as “Unit 1 - 01”. Within the noun 挨拶」, the kanji 「挨」with the label Unit 1-01 is the PRIMARY kanji of the noun, and so this noun is labelled “Unit 1-01” and is tied to the kanji in the database.
  • POS
    • Inside POS, there are two sections.
      • POS:
        • Refers to the primary Part of Speech for the given TERM.
      • POS Note:
        • Additional Parts of Speech for the given TERM, as well as notes on usage of the TERM.
    • We have the following “Parts of Speech” and POS Notes in our system:
      • K - Kanji
      • N - Nouns
      • V - Verbs
        • 一段 - Ichidan Verbs
        • 五段 - Godan Verbs
          • Note: For Godan-type verbs, we included which okurigana the root verb ends in.
            • For example, 飲む ends in む。We would include it like this: V(五段 - む).
        • する - Verbs that utilize “suru”
          • For example, 勉強する.
        • POS Note: TRANS - Transitive Verbs (他動詞 - i.e., to drop something)
        • POS Note: INTRANS - Intransitive Verbs (自動詞 - i.e., something falls)
      • ADJ - Adjectives
        • い - Adjectives that end in い.
        • な - Adjectives that end in な.
        • の - Adjectives that modify a noun. (Examples here).
        • たる - たる Adjectives (Explanation and examples here ).
      • OTHER - Others
        • ADV - Adverb
          • ADV (と) - An adverb that utilizes と.
            • For example, ゆっくりと寝てください.
        • IDIOM (4K) - Four-character Idioms
        • # - Number
        • EXP - Expression
        • PRONOUN - Pronoun
        • CONJ - Conjunction
    • KANJI MEANINGS
      • A concise meaning given for the “Primary” kanji (the kanji being focused on) and the “Sub” kanji (any other kanji present in the term). Used for mnemonic creation.
    • KANJI SURVEY INFORMATION
      • The percentage of surveyed Japanese citizen who knew the meaning of the kanji used in our questionnaire.
      • Note: This information is not present for kanji taken from the Jōyō Chart (常用表) document.
    • TERM SURVEY INFORMATION
      • The percentage of surveyed Japanese citizen who knew the meaning of the word in our questionnaire.
      • Note: This information is not present for vocabulary taken from the Jōyō Chart (常用表) document.
    • NOTES
      • Any miscellaneous information that has information to support the learning of the kanji, its readings or any of its related vocabulary. May also include additional meanings for those meanings which exceeded our 60-character limit.
